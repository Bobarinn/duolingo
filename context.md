Background & Context for Prototype / Workflow Demo

The product being analyzed is Duolingo, a learning platform best known for language education but increasingly expanding into other skill domains such as math and chess. The company’s mission is to make education accessible and engaging by turning learning into a daily habit through short, gamified lessons.

In 2025, Duolingo launched a chess learning course, designed to teach beginners the fundamentals of chess through short puzzle-based exercises and guided gameplay. The course quickly became the fastest-growing course in Duolingo’s history, reaching over one million active users within its first 30 days and growing to millions of users by late 2025.

The chess course is structured similarly to Duolingo’s language courses: learners complete short interactive exercises that teach concepts progressively while reinforcing daily practice through streaks, XP points, and levels.

One challenge learners face in the chess course is that when they make mistakes during puzzles or games, the system often shows the correct move but does not fully explain why the mistake occurred or what concept was missed. Because chess improvement relies heavily on pattern recognition and understanding tactical ideas, learners benefit significantly from feedback that explains the reasoning behind correct moves.

The proposed improvement introduces an AI-powered feature called Adaptive Chess Coach. This system analyzes mistakes, explains tactical concepts, and generates short follow-up puzzles designed to reinforce the concept the learner missed.

The goal is to transform mistakes into learning opportunities, helping learners recognize patterns faster and improve more effectively. The system would function similarly to a human chess coach, providing quick explanations and targeted practice after each mistake.

Slide 7 — Part 3: AI Workflow Design
AI Tool: Adaptive Chess Coach

The Adaptive Chess Coach analyzes incorrect moves during puzzles or gameplay.

When a learner makes a mistake, the system captures the board position and evaluates it using a chess engine.

The system identifies the missed tactic or strategic concept and classifies the error type.

A language model generates a short explanation describing why the move was incorrect and highlighting the correct tactical idea.

The system then generates a follow-up puzzle that reinforces the same concept.

This workflow helps learners understand patterns quickly and reinforces learning through immediate practice.

Instead of simply showing the correct move, the app functions like a personal chess tutor guiding the learner through mistakes.
